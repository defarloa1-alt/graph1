# what does dewey "dewey": "323", mean

In the Dewey Decimal Classification, the number **323** is the class for *civil and political rights*, often treated broadly as human rights in a political-science / social-science context.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3]

More specifically, within the 300s:

- 300s = Social sciences.[^1_2][^1_3]
- 320s = Political science.[^1_1]
- 323 = Civil and political rights (civil rights, human rights, citizenship, freedoms such as speech and assembly, etc.).[^1_4][^1_5][^1_2][^1_1]

So if you see `"dewey": "323"` in a record, it indicates the work is classified under civil and political rights in the Dewey system.

# can u do the full breakdown

Here’s how **323** fits into Dewey, and how it can be extended; after the base schedules, most of the real nuance comes from using tables (places, groups, etc.) with 323.

## Where 323 sits in Dewey

- 300 = Social sciences.[^2_1][^2_2]
- 320 = Political science (politics and government).[^2_3][^2_4]
- 323 = Civil and political rights (including civil liberties, human rights, citizenship rights, political freedoms).[^2_5][^2_4][^2_1][^2_3]

Within the 320s division:

- 321 = Systems of governments and states.[^2_1][^2_3]
- 322 = Relation of state to organized groups and their members.[^2_3][^2_1]
- 323 = Civil and political rights.[^2_5][^2_1][^2_3]
- 324 = The political process (elections, parties, campaigns, etc.).[^2_4][^2_1][^2_3][^2_5]
- 325 = International migration and colonization.[^2_1][^2_3][^2_5]
- 326 = Slavery and emancipation.[^2_3][^2_5][^2_1]
- 327 = International relations.[^2_6][^2_5][^2_1][^2_3]
- 328 = The legislative process.[^2_5][^2_1][^2_3]


## What 323 covers conceptually

323 is used for works on:

- Civil liberties and human rights in general (freedom of speech, religion, assembly, due process, privacy, etc.).[^2_7][^2_8][^2_1]
- Political rights such as voting, holding office, and participation in government.[^2_7][^2_1]
- Policies whose primary focus is civil rights, even if they touch other domains (e.g., education policy as civil rights is kept in 323, not in the 370s).[^2_7]

One guide explicitly notes: use 323 for policies with civil rights implications rather than other policy numbers.[^2_7]

## How 323 is expanded (decimals)

The core schedules for 323 get much of their detail via decimal expansion combined with standard and special tables. Two important examples from the technical documentation:

- 323.11 – Civil and political rights of ethnic and national groups (base for further subdivision).[^2_8]
- 323.1197260792 – Civil and political rights of Navajo Indians in Utah, where:
    - 323.11 = civil and political rights of ethnic and national groups.[^2_8]
    - 9726 = Navajo (from Table 5 for ethnic/national groups, as instructed at 323.111–323.119).[^2_8]
    - 0792 = further subdivision (e.g., geographic, biography aspect, etc., combined via tables).[^2_8]

Another example of internal structure:

- 323.352 – Young people, where the schedule notes: “Class here children; thus, civil liberties of children classed in 323.352.”[^2_8]

This illustrates the pattern: 323 + .3xx/.35x + table numbers = very specific rights-contexts for specific groups in specific places.

## Practical reading of “dewey: 323”

When you see something like `"dewey": "323"` in a record, and no further decimals:

- You can safely read it as: **general works on civil and political rights / civil liberties**, not tied to a specific country, group, or right.[^2_1][^2_3][^2_5]
- If you see `"323.3..."`, `"323.4..."`, etc., the decimals will usually encode:
    - particular groups (children, minorities, ethnic or national groups, etc., via 323.x plus Table 5),[^2_7][^2_8]
    - particular places (countries, regions, via geographic tables),[^2_7][^2_8]
    - sometimes form or special aspects (biography, legal treatment, history).

# what about lcc   "lcc": "JN",

In the Library of Congress Classification, an **LCC** code starting with **JN** means the work is about the **political institutions and public administration of Europe**.[^3_2][^3_5]

More precisely in the J-schedule:

- J = Political science (general).[^3_5][^3_2]
- JA–JS = General political science, political theory, political institutions in general.[^3_2][^3_5]
- **JN = Political institutions and public administration (Europe).** This covers things like national governments, constitutions, parliaments, executive structures, and related administrative institutions in European countries.[^3_5][^3_2]

So `"lcc": "JN"` on a record tells you that the item has been shelved in the *political science* discipline, specifically under *European political institutions/public administration*.

# doesnt it also have broader and narrower than, or is that fast

LCSH **does** have explicit broader/narrower relationships; FAST does too, but they use them a bit differently.

### In LCSH

In the **LCSH** authority record for a heading (like “Constitutional history”), you will typically see:

- **BT** = *Broader term* (one step up the hierarchy).[^6_1][^6_2][^6_3][^6_4]
- **NT** = *Narrower term* (one step down the hierarchy).[^6_2][^6_3][^6_4][^6_1]
- **RT** = *Related term* (associative, not hierarchical).[^6_3][^6_5][^6_2]

These are encoded in the subject authority record and printed or displayed as cross-references; they let you walk up/down the conceptual hierarchy (e.g., Vehicles → Motor vehicles → Trucks → Dump trucks).[^6_6][^6_4][^6_2][^6_3]

So the LCSH authority for **sh85031326 / “Constitutional history”** will normally list BT/NT/RT references to related headings inside LCSH.

### In FAST

**FAST** is derived from LCSH and also has thesaural relations:

- FAST authority records include **Broader, Narrower, Related, Use for** links between FAST terms.[^6_7][^6_8][^6_9]

However:

- FAST is **faceted and post-coordinate**, so relationships are managed per facet (topical, geographic, form, etc.), and you don’t get the same compound-subdivision structure as in full LCSH.[^6_8][^6_9][^6_7]

In your graph, if you’re resolving **LCSH IDs** like `sh85031326`, the BT/NT are natively LCSH relationships; if you’re pulling **FAST** (fst…) IDs, those broader/narrower links are coming from FAST’s own authority records, even though the labels are largely inherited from LCSH.
